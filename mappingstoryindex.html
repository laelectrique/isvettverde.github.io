Rats, Rats, Everywhere!

By: Isvett Verde

Poking around subway tracks, scurrying out of garbage cans – rats seem to be everywhere in New York City. Native to China and Mongolia, the brown rat (Rattus norvegicus) can be found almost anywhere you’ll find humans around the world. Before they became pizza-eating city-dwellers, they fed on wild plants and small animals on cold, open plains.

Finding a reliable food supply in their midst, they eventually switched to living on farms and in villages. A recent study revealed that they first traveled to southeastern Asia, before a group of rats spread northeast into Japan and Siberia. Another group headed west, reaching Europe in what appear to have been three major arrivals on the Continent. These rats may have traveled on overland routes tucked away in carts or carriages, or perhaps stowed away on ships that sailed along the coasts of Asia and Europe.

After spreading slowly for thousands of years, the scientists found, brown rats have made their way all over much of the planet in just the past three centuries.

New York City Department of Health is waging the most intense battles against rats. According to the NYC Department of Health and Mental Hygiene, buildings, sidewalks, parks and sewers in rat reservoirs are targeted for extra exterminator visits and intensified enforcement from health inspectors. Since the program was launched in 2015, officials say rat reservoirs are working.

“I don’t think we’ll get rid of them.”

Brooklyn residents say otherwise. According to Michael Williams – a 60 year old show owner, that has lived in Bed-Stuy his whole life – the rat issue has gotten worse in the past 3 years. “I don’t think we’ll get rid of them,” he said.
